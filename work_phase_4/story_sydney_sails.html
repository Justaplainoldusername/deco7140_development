Sydney Sails

Ah, mate, I tell ya — there’s just something about the sea that never leaves your blood. I might be seventy-two now, but every time I sit down at the workbench and start shaping a bit of timber into a hull, I can smell the salt air again, hear the gulls, and picture those big old yachts cutting across Sydney Harbour. When I was a young bloke, I’d stand there for hours just watching ’em. Sleek, graceful things — full of adventure and heartbreak, too. Building these ships now, it’s like bringing those memories back to life, piece by piece, plank by plank. Keeps me connected to who I was, and who I still am, deep down.

I got into model building pretty late in life — late fifties or so. Figured I’d give it a go after retirement when the hands weren’t too shaky and the missus reckoned I needed something to keep me out of her hair. Well, I was hooked from the first one. There’s a certain peace in it, you know? Just me, the tools, the smell of wood and glue, and a pot of tea on the bench. It’s patient work — slow, steady — and that’s the beauty of it. When you finally stand back and see her taking shape, all the fiddly little hours make sense.

The Le Superbe’s my latest beast. She’s a big one — a proper challenge, and I love that. Mantua Models really know how to test an old fella’s patience. But that’s half the fun, isn’t it? Every time I sit down with her, I reckon I’m learning something new. The engineering side of me gets a kick out of figuring out the rigging, or getting the symmetry just right. There’s a bit of pride in knowing you built something so intricate with your own two hands — a real sense of accomplishment that no screen or gadget could ever give ya.

What makes it even better these days is sharing it with my granddaughter. She’s dead-keen on model building just like her old pop here. Sharp as a tack, that one — steady hands, curious mind. We’ll spend hours together tinkering away, her asking questions and me trying to sound like I know all the answers. Watching her fall in love with the craft, seeing that spark in her eyes, well — that’s better than launching any ship. I reckon she’ll carry on the hobby long after I’ve hung up the magnifying glasses.

At the end of the day, building these ships isn’t just about boats. It’s about memory, patience, and keeping the old ticker ticking — both the heart and the mind. When I’m at the bench, I’m not some old retiree with dodgy knees; I’m a young fella again, dreaming of the sea, thinking about Dad out on his fishing boat, and the smell of salt on the wind. These little ships, they’re stories — my stories, my family’s stories — and every time I build one, I feel like I’m adding another chapter. Wouldn’t trade it for the world, mate.
