Ethics Response

For as long as we have been designing websites, we have ensured that we put ethics at the leading edge of all our design decisions. The ACS Code of Professional Ethics provides us with our guidelines for such design via the three values of honesty, trustworthiness and respect. It is through these three filters that we pass all our client needs and design approaches to ensure that ethical design is at the heart of every site we create. Here you will find the results of our ethics analysis including our feedback and recommendations for your website.

Introduction

These days users are no longer expected to be passive consumers of a website's content. Instead they are invited to interact with sites via logins, comments, contributions and surveys, to name a few. In all instances, this leads to a situation whereby the website owner possesses user data, which requires careful consideration of the ethics of obtaining and holding such data. As your website will be collecting and holding user data to enable users to interact with your site via the Newsletter Signup Form, we wish to provide you with some recommendations that will help ensure your website follows ethical design principles, and is an honest, trustworthy and respectful place for your community of users.

Our Ethical Reasoning

At the beginning of the project we were provided a comprehensive brief which included the required fields for the Newsletter Signup Form. We divided these fields into two groups: those fields necessary for a user to successfully sign-up to a newsletter (Full name, email), and additional fields that are not required (remaining fields). From here we assessed these groups using the three values of honesty, trustworthiness and respect.

Honesty

In the ACS Code of Professional Ethics, honesty includes the practice of being open and truthful in all interactions, and ensuring there is no representation of action, situation or capability.

Necessary Fields

These fields are the minimum required for users to sign up to websites and newsletter subscriptions and users expect that these fields will be requested of them for these purposes. It is not necessary to explain why we are requesting these details, therefore, we have assessed these fields to be presented with openness and truthfulness, and so long as this data is only used for the newsletter subscription there is no misrepresentation of how the data will be used.

Additional Fields

These fields are not a requirement for a Newsletter Signup Form and users will be unaccustomed to providing this information for a newsletter signup. Additional information as to why these fields are being included in this form, and how the data will be used, is a prerequisite for this section to be considered open, truthful and accurately represented and therefore pass the Honesty value. The risks associated with not including this additional information include user distrust of the form, which will lead to lower engagement and signups, and user distrust of the website as a whole.

Trustworthiness

Trustworthiness invites us to respect a person‚Äôs privacy and confidentiality, and to act with integrity when holding information or data related to a person.

Necessary Fields

By collecting only the field required to subscribe a user to a newsletter, we are proving to our user that we respect their right to privacy and elevating our perception of trustworthiness. By including a short statement near the form announcing our intentions to use this daily for the sole purpose of subsribing them to the newsletter, we will further elevated this perception.

Additional Fields

While these additional fields could provide useful data on the lifestyles and preferences of your users, the information users are being invited to share is private and sensitive in nature. Special care must be taken to ensure the user‚Äôs information is held with integrity, and their privacy and confidentiality respected. Ensuring these fields are optional (that is, the form can be submitted without these fields being completed) allows users agency over what information is provided through the form which respects their privacy. Information on how data will be encrypted both in transit and at rest should also be provided so that users can be assured of their privacy and confidentiality. De-linking identifiable user data (such as Full name) with the additional fields also offers greater confidentiality of the user data. The risks of making these additional fields mandatory is low completion rates of the form, or inaccurate data as users protect their privacy by providing false information in order to complete the form. The risks of making the additional fields optional is low completion rates of these fields, lessening the scope of your data collection. The risks of collecting and storing this data are the breaches of privacy and confidentiality that may occur with a data breach or misuse of the data, particularly if the data has not been anonymised. We propose using alternate methods of collecting this data (included in our Recommendations) that will enable these fields to pass the Trustworthiness value.

Respect

In the ACS Code of Professional Ethics, Respect asks that we identify and mitigate risks to others associated with our work.

Necessary Fields

Even though it is standard practice to request a user‚Äôs name and email address for website logins and newsletter signup forms, there is still a risk associated with collecting and holding this data. These risks include data breaches that may result in unsolicited email to the user, or misuse of the newsletter subscription list through internal practices or sale of the list to other entities. As we cannot eliminate these feels from the form, mitigating these risks includes hardening the data storage against data breaches, adhering to the Spam Act 2003, defining and following internal processes to ensure this data is used respectfully, and only allowing the data to be used for its intended process. With these strategies defined and followed, these fields will pass the Respect value.

Additional Fields

The field Sexuality falls within what the Privacy Act 1988 considers to be sensitive information. Sensitive information is afforded a higher level of protection than personal data due to its sensitive nature and the potential for misuse of this data to cause serious harm to the individual. In the event of a data breach, data linking an identifiable individual with their sexual orientation or practices could greatly impact their quality of life, including their ability to live in or travel to certain countries or their ability to access adequate and appropriate care. The remaining fields are considered Personal data, which requires less protection but still carries risk for the user. Malicious actors adept at social engineering may gain access to an individual‚Äôs banking accounts with name, occupation, and credit limits alone. Additionally, data on a user‚Äôs takeaway dining preferences could be used to target advertising campaigns to encourage or exacerbate existing behavioural patterns for commercial benefit. To mitigate these risks, we propose using alternate methods of collecting this data (included in our Recommendations) that will enable these fields to pass the Respect value.

Our Recommendations

To minimise risk to our users and maximise the ethical design of the website, we recommend that the Newsletter Signup Form contains only those fields necessary to function (full name and email address). This ensures that the data you retain is of the lowest risk should a breach occur, and conforms with what users expect of a signup form elevating user trust and website usability.

We appreciate your desire to collect data from your users that better enables you to understand your community. For this, we recommend engaging the services of third-party-providers whose speciality is in market research and community surveys. Reputable companies in this arena are adept at structuring surveys to capture the data that suits your purposes, can anonymise data to ensure user privacy and confidentiality, and store data in locations hardened against cyberattacks and data breaches.

Common Questions

We have worked with numerous clients on websites similar to yours. Here is a collection of frequently asked questions about our processes and recommendations that you may find relevant.

Our budget won‚Äôt cover third-party-providers. What are our other options?

We understand that not every organisation has the budget to engage Market Researchers to conduct community surveys. In these instances, we recommend taking the approach of an in-house, optional, anonymous user survey through either the website, or a lower-cost provider such as Survey Monkey. Key considerations when taking this approach are:

  • A clear strategy as to what needs to be achieved through the collection of this data, and what questions need to be asked to achieve this goal.
  • Openness as to why the data is being collected. Users are often enthusiastic about engaging in surveys when there is a high level of existing trust with the website or brand and they know how their data will be used.
  • Secure data storage that is hardened against cyber attacks and established policies on how a data breach will be handled and communicated to those affected including how to engage cybersecurity lawyers in the event of a ransomware attack. When choosing cloud storage, it is important that you are familiar with the privacy laws of the country where the data will be stored to ensure it meets with Australian standards.

For many clients we found that the cost of developing the form in-house with adequate data storage and protection against cyberattacks, was comparable with a Market Research approach, and the peace-of-mind of using the latter was worth the additional expense.

Can we redirect the Newsletter Signup Form to a third party that can collect the additional fields as well?

Yes, this is technically possible. However, we encourage our clients to keep the Newsletter Signup Form simple and within the website rather than navigating the user away from the site. Our reasoning behind this is:

  • Trust. When users have established a level of trust with your website, their barriers to submitting data to a form are lowered. Redirecting a user to a form that is outside your site requires that they re-establish trust with this new platform. If this trust is not established, the user will abandon the form.
  • Branding. Branding is about consistency and trust. While the user is within your website, you have full control over the branding and appearance of the site. However, an external site may not offer the same flexibility, and your branding and imagery may not be consistent with your primary site. Again, this impacts trust.
  • Lowering user engagement with your site. Generally, as website owners, we want our users to be on our site for as long as possible. Directing them to an external site may end their session on yours, whereas keeping them within the site ensures they are continuing to engage with your content.
